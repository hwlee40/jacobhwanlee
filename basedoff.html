What is Basedoff?
Each year in South Korea more than 100,000 individuals enlist into the Republic of Korea Army (ROKA) to carry out their mandatory military service. In 2019, I was one of the 100,000 who reluctantly enlisted for the military service. Before long, I was assigned a date and base where I would be receiving bootcamp training. I was, of course, curious about this base where I would be spending my next 1.5 years at. And carrying out my research online, I was quick to abandon the catastrophic mess that is the ROKA homepage and ended up scrapping bits of information here and there from online forums and blogs.

Back then, I took it for granted that practical information about the military service could not be found online. But as I talked to more people, I realized that this issue was common amongst other enlistees who had also come to the military not knowing much of what to expect.

So if the ROKA won’t provide the information necessary for future enlistees, why not make one for them myself. Basedoff is an information platform for future enlistees who deserve to know what to expect when enlisting into the military.
The Problem
How can I redesign the research process of future enlistees to allow them to easily find the right kind of information based on their needs?

Research
Testing Initial Assumptions
To test my initial assumptions, I sent out a survey to 6 of my platoonmates and 6 friends from South Korea. The survey results mirrored my initial impression of how individuals have little access to any practical information about the military.
Q1:
Do you feel that ROKA has provided you with enough information regarding your military service?
Only 2 of the 12 participants thought the ROKA provided enough information regarding one’s military service. That’s just 16.6% of the participants!
Q2:
Is practical information regarding the military service easily accessed online?
5 of the 12 participants answered yes to the question. Although the numbers are higher than the initial question, this is still only 41% of the participants.
In-Depth Interviews
Following the initial surveys, I conducted 1:1 interviews with six future enlistees to get a deeper understanding of how they were conducting research about the military service.
15~20 minute interviews were conducted over the phone. I prepared a couple of questions beforehand and guided the conversation to focus on the way enlistees had trouble finding information they wanted to find.
Interview Synthesis
I used an affinity diagram to synthesize the interview findings. From the diagram, I was able to gather some insight on the main pain points enlistees were facing as well as an overview the tools & processes they were using when conducting research.
The ROKA Homepage
The interview revealed that one of the main curiosities future enlistees had was about military specialties. I took a look at how the official ROKA homepage was providing information regarding specific military specialties and highlighted some of the problems I noticed. I did not expect much to begin with but was surprised to find out how little information was being provided.
Research Insights
01.
Information regarding the military service is too generic.
Individuals had a general idea of how the military worked but were clueless about particular subjects such as military specialties & bases.
02.
It’s difficult to tell if information found online is trustworthy.
Even when individuals were able to find information regarding a specific topic, much of it was from unreliable sources.
03.
People have a bad perception of the military in general.
Even before having enlisted, most individuals viewed the military in a negative light.

Ideation
Research to Ideation
Based on each of the three main research insights, I brainstormed potential solutions to help guide the following ideation process. Not all of the initial ideas were implemented but all served as a rough framework for when I was drafting initial experience ideas.
1. Information regarding the military service is too generic.
    ● Provide information based on individual’s specific needs.
    Onboarding experience that is personalized.
    User prompted to provide specialty & base information. Information is catered to fit the user.
    ● Have more information in the first place.
    Allow anyone to post & make it easy to post.
    Promote sharing information/knowledge.
2. It’s difficult to tell if information found online is trustworthy.
    ● Let people know clearly which information is trustworthy.
    Perhaps a small team to go around approving posted information?
    Users could send proof about their military service and apply to be verified users.
    ● Friends or other users can verify information.
    Threads can have a voting system where users can upvote helpful & trustworthy information.
3. People have a bad perception of the military in general.
    ● Break from conventional military imagery.
    3D illustrations?
    Play with military colors.
Sketching Rough Ideas
Using the ideas generated, I thought of three potential ways I could implement these solutions as an actual experience. Considering both the pros & cons of each option, I ended up combining the first two ideas for the first wireframe.
Further Research
Research for a New User Group
At this point in the process, a new group of users had to be addressed in order for the first idea in the sketch to function—the users posting information. I held short interviews with 3 platoon mates who were active posters on DC Inside/Naver. In doing so, I was able to gain insights on how to motivate users to share information regarding their military experience and what frustrated them most when sharing information online.
01.
Recognition fuels participation.
Users were posting information expecting to be recognized for the information they provided. To recognize and reward users for participating, I opted for a simple upvote, comment, and notification system.
02.
Ease of access.
Reducing the number of steps it takes to share information makes sure users are not discouraged from sharing information due to a convoluted posting process.

User Testing
With a workable low-fidelity prototype at hand, I conducted 4 contextual inquiry sessions followed by a brief interview with my platoon mates to reveal any problems with the prototype.
User Test Insights
01.
It is difficult to discover specialties.
The suggestions appearing on the home screen were rarely used. Because these suggestions were of specific specialties, it was less appealing for people not necessarily interested in the specific specialty that was being presented. For the final prototype, I opted for a version where themes were suggested and users could click on the theme they were interested in to be presented with a set of specialties that fit the theme.
02.
The point system is overly emphasized.
The point system was too emphasized and ended up distracting the users from achieving their red route task: reading about and reviewing different specialties. I decided to adjust the UI of the point system to provide more clarity on what the points were reflecting (likes, views, comments) and to remove the social media aspect of Basedoff to reduce unnecessary rabbitholes on the app.
03.
No one was verifying their account.
Having to verify your account in the account page just to post verified reviews was too much to ask of the user. To keep things simple and promote more verified reviews, I decided to allow users to verify their posts straight as they were making reviews.
Problems with the UI
Participants also revealed several UI problems that became obvious after being pointed out.
● The button on the bottom of the screen in the specialties page is unpleasant on android.
● The button on the bottom of the screen in the specialties page is not labeled + unclear.
● Verify button in accounts page is not labeled + unclear.
● Specialty numbers in the specialty page is unclear without any labels.
● No option to change account password.
● On specialties accepting applications, add link to the page where people can actually apply.
● Sign-up option missing from login screen.

Usability Testing
80% Task Completion Rate
To measure task completion rate, I asked 5 participants to leave a review for a specialty and ask a question about a specialty. 4 of the 5 participants were able to accomplish said tasks unassisted in under 10 minutes.
62.5% User Satisfaction
I let 5 participants interact with the final prototype before having them answer two multiple choice questions: How would you describe your experience with the app? Would you recommend the app to a friend? Assigning each choice a point from 1~100 (e.g. very satisfied = 100, satisfied = 75, neutral = 50, unsatisfied = 25, very unsatisfied = 0) I took the mean score of the answers provided to measure user satisfaction.
With my final prototype of Basedoff, I interviewed 5 friends to measure user satisfaction and success rate of the prototype.
Evaluation
Takeaways
Basedoff being one of the first projects where I took insights gathered from users to actual design choices, it taught me a lot about the user research process. I paid more attention than ever to each design chocie I was making and if it aligned with the findings from my research.

Working on solving a problem I personally experienced was very rewarding. That being said, I found myself at times making assumptions whilst designing due to the mindset that I was also a potential user for Basedoff. Though I was able to pick out and fix a several of these design choices made on assumptions through user tests in between the steps of the project, I am certain there are ones I missed that could make Basedoff less usable for its primary user group. Making design choices on assumption is something I want to pay closer attention to in future projects.
For the Future
Though user satisfaction was 62.5%, I am confident I can shoot for a higher number if I tweak the visual elements of my app. Some of the feedback I recieved from the final round of user tests were that the app seemed a bit crowded. I want to work on establishing a clearer visual hierarchy in the specialties page to solve this issue in the future.

Additionally, most of the decisions made for Basedoff have been based on the insights gathered through my initial user interviews in the research stage of the project. However, an appropriate next step would be to further test these findings and validate the UX choices that I have made in response to my research insights. Post launch, measuring retention rate by collecting data on how often users use certain features on the app will help validate said insights.
